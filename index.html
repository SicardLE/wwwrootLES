Mom


Her favorite meal was pork chops (even more than steak). When out at a restaurant she would eat about 1/2 of her meal then say she was full. When the waitress came by and asked if anyone wanted dessert, my mom never refused it.

Dad


My dad was born right-handed. When he was young (I`m guessing 7 or 8 years old) he broke his right arm while imitating one of his movie cowboy heroes, Tom Mix. He had placed his `skippy wagon` at the bottom of the steps of his grandmother`s porch - pretending it was a horse that he ran up to and jumped on. Prompt (pg 13): Activities my family participated in together

I was in 3rd grade when my dad thought we should try camping. He bought these old, wool sleeping bags from a nearby army surplus store. We went to a town in Vermont called White River Junction and camped one night on some public land along the White River (or maybe it was the Connecticut River because the town is where the White River joins the Connecticut River). We slept in the sleeping bags inside our station wagon. We liked it and the following year my dad invested in camping supplies: a big canvas tent, more comfortable sleeping bags, and everything else we needed. My dad, mom, brother and I camped together until I moved off to college.

We spent many of my dad`s 2-weeks summer vacation at the campground of Hammonasset Beach State Park in Madison, Connecticut - right on the ocean. This was our favorite camping spot, but other years we went to Lake Champlain in New York State, Fundy National Park on the Bay of Fundy in New Brunswick with day trips including driving to parts of Nova Scotia. My dad wanted to take the ferry to Prince Edward Island but we missed the ferry the day we went. He always wanted to see the sights of PEI and the red sand beaches but we never got to.

My dad also bought a boat - a 16 foot fiberglass tri-hull - for fishing and water skiing. When we were not camping, we would go fishing, skiing, and picnicking on the Connecticut River (above the Holyoke dam where the water was a little cleaner) or various lakes, and sometimes fishing in the ocean (usually launching from Niantic, Connecticut).



From Left: Lorraine (Mom), Bill, Jimmy, me, Roy (Dad), and Brian.

In the fall of the year we often would go on Sunday rides through the mountains and sometimes stop at shops along highway 2 and pick up a bushel of Macintosh apples, cherries, or maple sugar candy.

Prompt (pg 17): What summers were like growing up
Summer vacation was almost always spent outside (weather permitting). I spent many hours and miles on my bike and often hung out with the half dozen friends in my neighborhood and my brother. I did not get involved with any organized sports, but my friends played a lot of pick-up baseball or football. Hot days were spent at the pool; but hotter days would find us in the `Willow` movie theater - a couple blocks away - to see a couple cartoons and a double feature movie - all for 25 cents.
Prompt (pg 18): A childhood memory that stands out
I was between 2 and 3 years old and lived in a duplex on Elcon Drive in Willimansett, Massachusetts with mom and dad. I often watched my dad before he went to work. I remember seeing him shave with a safety razor. He would lather up his face with shaving cream and I watched as he would scrape the foam and whiskers from his face. On the weekends he would not get up quite as early. One Saturday in particular my dad woke up and he could hear a little noise of someone in the bathroom.

I had walked into the bathroom and saw his razor and tried to shave. I held the blade to my cheek and was ready to make the first swipe of imaginary whiskers. Before I could pull the razor down my cheek my dad grabbed my wrist and pulled the razor away. I had a little cut that I`m told left a scar that I could never find when I got old enough to shave - which was about 12 or more years later.

Dad said that the scar would have probably been a lot more noticeable (and painful) if he had not caught me when he did.

Prompt (pg 22): Activities I enjoyed

When I was young, I always tried to ice skate in the winter, but I never got the knack of it and could barely move around on the ice. When I was a senior in high school and the winter temperatures dropped enough, I was asked to help water the outdoor skating rink. I did not have skates at that time so I just watched my classmates play hockey.

One of the freshmen was a very tall guy that went on the ice using his brand new skates for the first time that Winter of 1970. After a few minutes he fell on the ice and broke his leg. That was the end of his skating for that year.

I was one of the guys helping him off the ice and I noticed that his skates were just my size. I later asked him if I could borrow them. He agreed to lend them to me. I gave skating a new try with a new plan. I was going to attempt to skate with a hockey stick in my hand. Little by little I took to the ice and focused on the puck instead of my skating. In less than a week I was speeding around the rink - skating forwards and backwards like I never had before.

I was not the best skater (I dare say I was at least average) but I could hold my own on the ice and was good enough to compete with my classmates.

Prompt (pg 30, b): The friends I have known the longest and how we met

Chris

I went to a minor seminary high school in Fall of 1967. It was run by the Catholic priests and brothers of Blessed Sacrament religious order. This boarding school was in the town of Hyde Park, New York (a little North of the city of Poughkeepsie, NY). It was roughly 100 miles from my home in Chicopee, MA. One of my classmates was Chris Pariseau. I thought of him as having these qualities: friendly, athletic, loud, short (sorry Chris). After 4 years of high school, we both continued on with the Blessed Sacrament religious at the novitiate house in Barre, MA (which was only about 50 miles East of Chicopee). He spent the usual 2 years there and advanced to the Blessed Sacrament major seminary in Cleveland, Ohio. I remained in Barre for another 1 1/2 years. I saw him a couple times while he was in Cleveland. On one of those visits (I believe it was Summer of 1974), I met a couple friends of his that we played tennis with. One of those girls was named Paula.

Chris did not stay in the seminary. I did not see him after I left the novitiate in Barre and moved to St. Mary Parish in Menasha, Wisconsin in January of 1975.

My focus with the Blessed Sacrament community (who staffed the parish and some of whom were on the teaching staff of St. Mary High School) was to complete my college studies here. Because of low enrollment in Cleveland, OH, the seminary was closed. My major seminary studies were to take place in Menasha instead. Another of my duties was to assist Father Roland Laliberte with church liturgies. This was to give me the experience of parish life for a priest. I decided to leave the Blessed Sacrament order in the Spring of that year. However, I did not leave Menasha. Instead I decided to complete college and graduated from, St. Norbert College in May of 1977. That was not the only reason I decided to stay in Wisconsin, but that is another story which is another chapter for this journal.

A few years after I graduated, I heard from Chris. He had joined the Navy and was in basic training at Great Lakes Naval Base (near Milwaukee, I think). Kate and I decided to try visiting him but when we got there, he had already shipped out. I did not hear from him after that.

Skip ahead 22 years

A conversation with one of my friends from Menasha prompted me to think about Chris and one day while working as a programmer at Kimberly-Clark Corporation, I was using a web site to research a software vendor. I thought `If I can find a company with a web search, why couldn`t I find a person?` I searched for Christopher Pariseau by name. The results were numerous. Even when I limited it to the state of Massachusetts there were about 6 hits with that name. I noticed that one of the results listed a phone number under the name `Christopher and Paula Pariseau`. I thought: could it be that Chris married the Paula from Cleveland?

I got up the nerve (aka a can of beer) to call the number. What sounded like a young teenage girl answered the phone. I gave her my name and explained why I was calling - telling her that I was searching for my friend Chris and asking if she would give him the message. If he did not know me, then I was sorry to be a bother. She said he was not home at that time, but she would give him the message.

The next day, Chris called me back. It was he! We talked for a while and said that someday we would need to get together, catch up with our lives, and introduce our families. Within the next few years we visit Chris and his family at his home while we took our kids to see the sights of Washington DC. Chris and Paula also visit us in Neenah, WI. We have not traveled to his home since, but we keep in touch by phone, mail, and Facebook. One thing I noticed about SOME friends: when you don`t see someone for many years, they give off a vibe and a look at you as if you are a stranger again. That was not the case with Chris; we were glad to see each other and it felt as if we were apart only days (instead of 22 years).



Prompt (pg 31): Friends who have been there for me when I needed them most

Dave Thiede

I first met Dave when joined the Blessed Sacrament (SSS) order at the Novitiate in Barre, MA in 1971. He was from Menasha, Wisconsin and had just finished his service in the US Army in Vietnam. After Novitiate, Dave moved to the SSS house in Old Town, Maine for a couple years. In the meantime, I finished at the Novitiate and moved to St. Mary Parish in Menasha, WI. I had left the SSS order and after a time heard that Dave left the SSS order as well, and then he returned to Menasha to live with his folks at their home on London Street also in Menasha.

Dave was always a good friend and we hung out often. The greatest test of our friendship occurred after I married Kathy Stanton on May 5, 1978. As Christmas 1978 approached, I needed to do serious shopping for a woman for the first time in my life. Dave stopped by our house one afternoon in early December and it had just started lightly snowing. I asked Dave: `Would you go to the store with me? I need to do a little shopping.` He said `Sure.` Now, that is a good friend!

But wait...there is more

Dave drove me in his car and we went to the Treasure Island store on College Ave, Appleton, WI. I had some ideas on what I was looking for as Christmas presents for my wife. I told Dave that I wanted to get a full length nightgown. Since, like I said earlier, I never shopped for a woman before and knew nothing about lady`s sizes. What I did know was that Dave and Kathy were exactly the same height, 5` 2”. I asked `Dave, come over here for a minute.` I held a nightgown I picked from the rack up to his neck. It took a couple tries to find the right size.

Just then, I noticed a lady had stopped nearby and looked at us in an odd way - a look that seemed like a mixture of surprise and maybe a little of disgust - before she continued to walk by. I put my choice of nightgown in the cart. Next: on to the rack of bathrobes...

Dave`s height came in handy, and I used him to measure bathrobe lengths as well. It looked like a good choice so I placed it in the cart. When I looked up, the same lady walked by again: not saying a word but still having that same look on her face. We walked toward the store entrance but I still wanted to get one more gift: perfume. I didn`t know any perfumes by brand name so I picked up a tester bottle and sprayed a little on my wrist. I didn`t like that one. Tried the next one by spritzing my inner elbow and thought that one was too strong. After spraying every possible spot on each of my arms with about 1/2 dozen tester bottles or more, I ran out of places to test. I said `Dave, would you come over here for a minute?` I tried a few more samples on his arms and finally found one that I liked that smelled pretty good. I looked up to tell Dave that I was ready to check out and...yes, that same lady happened to walk by again, shook her head and walked off.

We walked to the parking lot, got in the car, and started to leave. It was still cold outside but Dave said `Open your window ` and as he opened his window exclaimed `It smells like a French whorehouse in here.`

Kathy seemed to enjoy her gifts so I considered my first-time Christmas shopping a success.

The following year, Dave had stopped by the house to visit. I asked if he would go Christmas shopping with me again. He said `HELL NO!`

Prompt (pg 37): How and when we met

Kathleen Mary Stanton

I moved from the Blessed Sacrament Novitiate in Barre, Massachusetts to Menasha, Wisconsin on January 15, 1975 when I was 21 years old. I drove alone on interstate highway 90 to Chicago, IL and turned North, traveling another 3 1/2 hours or so on highway 94, then on 41 after passing through Milwaukee, Wisconsin, and finally reaching Menasha.

Over the next few days getting to know the Blessed Sacrament priests and brothers who staffed St. Mary Parish, I was introduced to some of the local people who were parishioners. I had a couple weeks to use for meeting locals, getting to know what everyone was involved in: some priests staffed the parish (Frs. Louis Prefontaine, Bob Lussier, and Roland Laliberte), others were teachers at St. Mary High School (Frs. Mike Noreika and Norman Pelletier). Brother Edmund Hardy was the cook. I believe Fr. Rob Baldoni was a resident of the SSS community but was retired.

For the rest of the month of January (until the Spring 1975 semester began) I would serve at daily morning mass. I met and was introduced to several parishioners who attended and got to know a few. One of the couples was Bud and Gladys Stanton. They were very friendly and I would speak to them occasionally after mass. Once I started classes at St. Norbert College in Depere, WI, I did not have time to serve daily mass so that I could travel and arrive in time for my first class.

I had been singing and playing guitar at masses prior to moving to Menasha (since 1967 when I was in the SSS Minor Seminary) so I inquired about how to join St. Mary`s choir (not the adult choir, but the guitar group). I found that the guitar group was primarily made up of the Phillips family and a few others - including Chuck Klein, Tom Klein, Kathy Stanton, etc.). I was welcomed into the group (without an audition) and would join them twice weekly: once on Wednesday evenings for a practice session, and then at 10:30 AM Sunday morning for the mass.

After having been with the group for a couple months and getting to know them better, some of the adult members invited me to go with them to participate in local activities. Chuck, Tom, Kathy, and I enjoyed the live bands in the area and eventually sought out our favorites - one of which was a band called `The Friends` which did a lot of 50`s and 60`s rock tunes and were fun to listen to.

Besides school, I tried to immerse myself in parish activities, especially assisting Fr. Roland Laliberte where I could. Brother Edmund also took me along to show me his ministry of visiting hospital patients, visiting shut-in people at their homes, etc. I did not regret doing any of that work but I decided in the end that it wasn`t the life that I wanted. So at the end of the school semester, I told the community that I was leaving the order. I would remain in Menasha until I graduated from St. Norbert but I would be getting a job, finding place to live, and moving out of the rectory.

I traveled home to be with my parents for a couple weeks and talk to them about my decision to leave the SSS order and my plans to complete college in Depere. When I returned I changed my major to Mathematics.

That is how we met.

Prompt (pg 47): How being a parent changed me (or why I don`t like to travel by airplane)

The first time I rode in an airplane I was about 14 years old. My Mom, Dad, my brother, and I were spending the day picnicking and water skiing on the Connecticut River near Hatfield, Massachusetts like we did many times during the summer. While sitting on the shore a small single-engine sea-plane landed on the river and pulled up to the shore not far from us. Brian and I followed my Dad who walked to the plane and spoke to the pilot briefly. Dad came back to us and asked if we wanted to go for a plane ride. Of course we said `Yes!`.

We got in and the plane started going up river - still on the water - until we went around a bend in the river. The pilot pushed the throttle lever to full and we were speeding up, bouncing on the waves, and heading right for the Hatfield Bridge. That was exciting! We cleared the bridge and went up and up.

The pilot asked us where we lived. We told him that we lived in Willimansett. In about 5 minutes or so he told us to look down and we could see our town. We flew around for about 20 or 30 minutes before we headed back to the river.

The landing was amazingly smooth (unlike the take-off). All I could think was that I loved flying: so much so that I thought I would join the Air Force some day so I could fly a faster plane, like the F-105 Fighter jet.

After I was married and started having children, I found it more and more difficult to step into an airplane. The fear of flying started slowly: at first I would only require a window seat to put me at ease; later it built into a feeling of dread where I would feel physically ill weeks before a scheduled trip; and finally, I could not step onto a plane and would travel by bus or train instead.

[FYI: This fear started well before the events of 2001-09-11.]

Prompt (pg 65): Three of my favorite foods as a child

I have had many `favorite` foods as a child but I`m limiting my top 3 to those that my Mom or Dad cooked from scratch. Here are they are.

1. Spaghetti - Mom made this recipe that she learned from a good friend - Annie Dupere (she was Italian, but her husband, John, was French). The recipe is simple, and my version is close to what my Mom made with these exceptions: she always browned and drained some ground beef in the kettle; I left the meat out and filled a glass of red wine and poured 1/2 of it into the sauce to neutralize the acidity of the tomatoes (and because the tomatoes did not like to drink alone).

2. Macaroni and Cheese - This recipe calls for elbow pasta, Velveeta brand cheese, and a 28 oz can of whole tomatoes. To save time, I use diced tomatoes. In a 9x13 inch pan, I use about 3 cups of uncooked elbows macaroni and most of a block of cubed Velveeta. I use Basil and a little pepper to season (no salt because the cheese is salty enough).

3. Ragout - I believe this is a French-Canadian recipe which is basically meat balls and pork Hocks in a thick, browned flour gravy. My Mom and Dad would work on its preparation together. Dad would sear about 1/2 dozen or so pork hocks in a skillet. Then he would remove the meat and put flour in same skillet and cook the flour until it was slightly browned. Mom would make lots of meat balls out of ground beef and pork (I think) with other spices. The meat balls would be cooked in the oven. Finally, the gravy would be made from the flour with more herbs and spices and pork hocks and meat balls added to the kettle where it would simmer for a long time until the hocks were tender.

The first meal we would have 1 pork hock each, a couple meat balls, and some gravy poured over boiled (or mashed) potatoes. The second meal would be just meat balls, potatoes and gravy (because the hocks were usually gone before the end of the first meal). Any subsequent meals were probably just gravy and potatoes.

This recipe I found gives you an idea of how the meat balls and gravy are made. It does not include the pork hocks. Check out this link:

https://www.yummly.com/recipe/French-Canadian-Ragout-de-Boulettes-_Meatball-Stew_-2352490#directions
Prompt (pg 65): Memorable meals my parents made when I was a child

There are probably others that I don`t mention, but here are a few:

- Pan fried steak in butter with a slice of bread browned in the steak-flavored butter;

- Fish Cakes - Tuna mixed with leftover mashed potatoes, shaped like hamburgers, browned in hot oil, and eaten with ketchup;

- Home-made French Fries - Mom cut up peeled potatoes with a crinkle knife. She would deep fry them in bacon fat she saved in a coffee can and put them in a brown grocery bag, salt them, and shake the bag to remove most of the grease. To this day, I have never tasted better French fries.

Note that we only had these every 2 or more months because it took that long to save enough bacon grease to fill the coffee can.

- Blood Sausage - As a kid, I never realized what the sausage was made of (in spite of its name) until I was a lot older. Still, that knowledge never deterred me from liking it. Mom served it with mashed potatoes and a veggie.

- Chuck Roast on the Hibachi (while camping) - During my Dad`s 2-week vacation in the summer we would go tent camping. He did ALL the cooking during that time. The most memorable of the meals he prepared was a 1 1/2 inch thick chuck roast that he cooked on a Hibachi charcoal grill. My Dad knew how to pick meat for tenderness and flavor and I remember those times we had this meal that the meat was as flavorful and tender as the most expensive cuts of meat at the best restaurants I visited.

- Dad`s Pancakes - My Dad made the best pancakes ever. I am sorry to say I did not inherit his recipe and have never had pancakes to surpass his. They were fluffy, had a unique taste, and big enough to fill a dinner plate. In my mind I can still see him at the stove with the cast iron skillet in his hand and flipping the pancake - without a using spatula - by tossing it 2 or 3 feet into the air.

For many years we played and sang together in church, around campfires, etc.: first - just the two of us; later - with our 7 children.

Prompt (pg 96): Most surprising or unique childhood vacation or outing

Starting when I was in 3rd grade, my dad began taking the family camping. We had a couple trial runs. The very first outing was sleeping overnight in the back of a station wagon in wool army sleeping bags next to the Connecticut River near Northfield, Massachusetts. The second outing was 2 nights near White River Junction, Vermont.

We all enjoyed it so my dad bought a tent and other gear - including better sleeping bags with air mattresses. After being better equipped, we started going to Hammonasett Beach State Park in Madison, Connecticut - which we did for 2 weeks, 8 years in a row.



The campground was about a 10 minute walk to the ocean/beach. After I was a couple years older, dad would allow Brian and I to got to the beach (not to swim alone) but most often to go to the boardwalk and concession stand to buy snacks or candy, catch fiddler crabs in the nearby tidal streams and just hang out. There were weekly, outdoor movies, and even an occasional dance. I think it was when Brian was 8 years old, we went to one of the dances: I listened to the music; Brian asked girls to dance (I was chicken; Brian was not).

One of the days the whole family spent at the beach getting some sun and swimming in the wavy water. When we got back to the campsite, my dad wanted to hang the beach towels to dry. There was only one tree on our site, but he noticed that there was a nail sticking up on the top of the outhouse, so he tied one end of the rope to the tree and the other to the nail.

Note: Each site had its own, one-hole outhouse that had a trap door in the back for access to the 5 gallon bucket for holding outhouse content. Twice a week a truck came through the campground to empty everyone`s bucket.

That night there was a rainstorm with strong winds. In the middle of the night we were all awakened with the sound of a loud crash. The four large beach towels were extra heavy with the rain and the wind caused the outhouse to tip on its side. My dad went out to investigate and was not very pleased to find the outhouse contents were all on the side wall. The truck - referred to as the `Honey Wagon` was not due to come until the next day; so the bucket was FULL. He spent at least an hour trying to clean up the mess as best he could.

Dad was not very happy but he learned not to tie a clothesline to that nail ever again. In subsequent years he would tell people the story but laughed at it.

Prompt (pg 117): Family members who have been in the military and/or public service

My dad, Roy C Sicard, was in the US Army around 1945 and later - his first “hitch” (assignment) was in what he called `Signal Corps`. I don`t know exactly what that entailed, but I believe it had to do with managing and delivering supplies. He was stationed in Trieste, Italy. He didn`t speak too much about WW2, but more about his life in that area. Some of the most memorable stories he told Brian and I included those about some of the girlfriends he had in Italy. Here are my top three stories.

1. He told me about the time he was socializing with some of his army buddies and their friends at an indoor roller rink. It was summer and warm and the doors to that building were open. After being there for a while my dad shouts to his buddies: “Follow me!”. He went outside and down the streets of the town until he and some of the others skating off the end of a dock and into the ocean.

2. PSA per my dad: He told me that one time he accidentally made a dinner date with two different girls on the same night at the same restaurant. When they both showed up - needless to say - they were both a bit angry and left him alone. He said he stayed at the restaurant and asked the waiter to continue to serve him with instructions that he did not want to see the bottom of his champagne glass to drown his sorrows. After a while he decided to leave but as soon as he opened the outside door and the fresh air hit him, he fell on his face. I imagine his army buddies helped him back to barracks on that occasion.

Prompt (not in journal): Fishing memories

1. When I was about 5 years old, my dad took me fishing for the first time in a boat. He got me up at about 3:00AM to have breakfast and get on the road to go to the Quabbin Reservoir in Massachusetts. He rented a boat and put his 10HP Mercury outboard on it and we were on the water before sun-up.

We were cruising to get to a different spot and in an instant I had the sensation of flying through the air and then getting wet. My dad described what happened (much later, when I was a little older) was the pin that actually holds the outboard to the bracket slipped half way off - causing the boat to rock violently and through me out of the boat.

Was my mom surprised to see me come home completely soaked.

2. I must have been about 7 years old when dad gave me a new fishing pole with a Mitchell 300 spinning reel just like his. I loved that pole and I still have it (but don`t use it anymore). One time after dinner, dad, Brian (who was about 4 years old), and I walked down our street. We were going fishing in the Connecticut River, just a short way north of the Willimansett Bridge.



Brian did not have a fishing pole at that time so dad rigged a stick with line and a hook and baited it for him and threw it in the river.

Dad and I did not get any bites; but Brian had something tugging at his line. Since Brian`s rig did not have a reel, my dad just told him to hold his pole and run away from the water. After about an hour, Brian had pulled in 7 suckers and one eel.

Moral of the story: In fishing, it is not always the gear that catches fish, but the patience.
